Interview with a Baker - Simon

By Kristen March 12, 2019

In our “Interview with a Baker” series, we ask artisanal bakers from across the globe with different backgrounds to answer twenty questions (Editor's note: some questions were deleted because they were already answered in other parts) about their experience with sourdough and baking in general. The questions the bakers answer range on topics from their baking history to specific techniques they use in their bread making process.

Hello Baker. Tell us a little about yourself, your background and who you are.

I am 54 years old, run a small building company in the UK, I am a father of two sons and a fantastic (!) husband to my lovely wife. I love contact sports and I love all rustic/natural methods of cooking. I have learnt lots of skills from you tube & have far too many hobbies. 

How can we find you on social media?

Instagram only @life_of_si

Do you have a website or blog or perhaps a video sharing platform you'd like to share?

No. 

How long have you been making bread, and specifically sourdough bread?

I have been making bread for around four years, sourdough around 8 months. 

What inspired you to explore sourdough?

Instagram (Editor's note: We both know who inspired you) & my fabulous sourdough family.

What has been the hardest part of learning how to make sourdough for you?

Hydration. 

What is your personal style with regards to making sourdough bread?

I don’t have any hard & fast rules and I am constantly trying different methods.

What’s your routine for refreshing and maintaining your starter?

Starter is kept in a kilner jar without the rubber seal, I keep it in the fridge from Sunday morning until Thursday morning, the starter would have been fed before it went into the fridge bottom shelf with 200g starter, 200g water & 200g flour, no hard and fast rule on which flour at present.
 

How soon before mixing your dough do you start your autolyse?

1.5 hours. Again no hard & fast rule; has been up to 4 hours.

What is your favorite grain to work with?

Really still experimenting but rustic country grains, but I really enjoy mixing grains & experimenting.

What is your prefered technique for developing gluten in your dough once it’s mixed together?

Definately stretch & fold.

Do you prefer the ambient or retarded method of baking?

Retarded all day long.

How long do you let your dough proof for?

This varies and takes some explaining, we run a log burner through the winter & our house is really warm, so I was using the no knead method for 18 hours, then shaping & placing in the banneton then refrigerated overnight to bake first thing in the morning but I found this left me with a bit of a weak loaf so now I’m now practicing autolyse for 1.5 hours, mixing my starter into the water, mixing the dough together & leaving to sit for 3 hours, then stretch & found every hour until I’m happy that the dough is full of energy.

What do you use to score your bread?

Lets take this question back a click or two. I was using an extremely sharp bread knife, then graduated to a builders knife (a Stanley knife) then for Christmas my wife bought me a plastic lame but I found it to be too flimsy, so last week I made one out of walnut & it’s perfect for me. I’m sure I’ll be sticking with it.
 

How do you bake your typical bread? (Dutch oven, steam oven, loaf pan…)

Dutch oven, loaf pan, pizza tray but my favourite being my outside brick oven directly on the oven floor, this is the skill I’m determined to master.

What is your favorite use for sourdough discard?

Trying to encourage others. I've giving loads to people in the sourdough world.

How do you see yourself expanding your sourdough skills in the future? Are there new skill you want to master?

I’m really trying to get my structure better, and I have a sneaking feeling that my starter flavour will improve as it ages, that’s what I’m really looking forward to.

What advice do you have for new bakers?

Don’t be afraid to ask, don’t be afraid to experiment, don’t be disheartened when you fail, but strut around like a peacock when you get it right (Editor's note: not that much old man).
 
Editor: If you follow both Simon and myself, you'll understand the references I've made. I refrained myself quite a bit.
