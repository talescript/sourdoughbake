Interview with a Baker - Kristen

In our “Interview with a Baker” series, we ask artisanal bakers from across the globe with different backgrounds to answer twenty questions about their experience with sourdough and baking in general. The questions the bakers answer range on topics from their baking history to specific techniques they use in their bread making process.

 
 
Hello, Kristen. Tell us a little about yourself, your background and who you are.
Hi, I’m Kristen, 38 years old. I work in a telecommunications field but bake in my spare time. As a kid I wanted to be a chef when I grew up. Cooking and baking have always been my favorite hobbies. 
 
How can we find you on social media?   
On Instagram, my profile is @the_dough_eyed_girl
 
Do you have a website or blog or perhaps a video sharing platform you'd like to share? 
Other than this one, I have a website for a local food swap here in my city. Alexander
built that site as well. 
 
 
Where in the world are you located?
I live in Wilmington, Delaware, US. 
 
What inspired you to explore sourdough? 
Well, I love bread. Good bread is not cheap. It’s also harder to find than it should be. So last year I started getting back into baking more, and along with that came pizzas, sticky buns, and bread. The more I baked, the more I was on Instagram looking at what other bakers were doing.  I got really interested in trying my hand at it. I was inspired by what I saw online. The crumb shots, and the scoring, and all the words that I had no idea what they meant, it fueled me to keep diving deeper. 
 
How long have you been making bread, and specifically sourdough bread?  
I made my first loaf of sourdough bread towards the end of 2018. It was very recent. I grew up with a mom who used to bake with me often. We made a few different kinds of breads with some regularity. We also used to make pizza when I was a kid from scratch a lot.
 
What has been the hardest part of learning how to make sourdough for you? 
Getting my starter to bloom in winter. I’m not really used to failing in the kitchen, but my first starter failed miserably. I’m not even going to lie, I was pretty annoyed about it. The problem I think was I complicated it. I kept seeing people making starters with apple juice or pineapple juice or kombucha. Guess what, that did not work for me at all. So, I stalked to bunch of bakers online and noticed most of them simply used water and flour and that worked out just fine. So my second attempt was 50/50 mix of flour and water and 11 days later, one thermal sock turned sourdough sleeping bag later, I had a live and active sourdough starter and was making my first loaf of bread. 
 
What is your personal style with regards to making sourdough bread? 
My style is still a little bit all over the place I think, but I guess we could call it experimental.  Because I’m still new to sourdough, I’m trying to improve so many skills and techniques, while simultaneously trying to experiment with many ingredients and flavor combinations in my head.
 
What’s your routine for refreshing and maintaining your starter? 
I feed my starter daily a mix 50/50 mix of water and flour. I use King Arthur All Purpose flour and their whole wheat flour in an even mix. I don’t keep a large amount of starter on hand. I’ve read many times over that 250 grams is the magic volume of starter to keep on hand that will live a long and happy life, but for now at least, I keep about 70 to 90 grams total. During the week I feed my starter once a day 30 grams of flour and 30 grams of water, but when I’m going to be making bread the next day I do a morning and a night time feeding. I also have not yet tried putting my starter in the refrigerator, but I do plan to move to that model in the next month or so. 
 
How soon before mixing your dough do you start your autolyse?
I don’t think I’m ever consistent with this. My first loaf of bread was a 30 minute autolyse and the bread I started today was a 4 hour autolyse. I do try to do them for about 2 hours or more because I’m always shocked and impressed at the amount of gluten that developed. 
 
What is your favorite grain to work with?  
I guess I’d have to say rye, but I don’t feel like I’ve tried enough kinds of wheat yet to be able to properly answer this. 
 
What is your preferred technique for developing gluten in your dough once it’s mixed together? 
My favorite may be coil folds, simply because I like the way the dough looks when I’m doing it. I think it makes a little dough butt and it makes me giggle every time like a child. Coil folds only work for me with higher hydration doughs, so if I’m working with a lower hydration dough, I do basic stretch and folds. 
 
Once I finish mixing the autolyse, starter and salt together, I do my first set of coil folds or stretch and folds about a half hour later. I repeat this again about 30 minutes later, then again for the third time about 45 minutes after, and the last set I do about an hour after that. 
 
Do you prefer the ambient or retarded method of baking? 
I have not actually tried the ambient method of baking. I really like letting the dough ferment overnight in the fridge. One of these days I’ll give it a try just so I can understand first hand the differences in flavor. 
 
How long do you let your dough proof for? 
It’s been a rather cold winter where I am, and I have not baked sourdough bread during the summer months yet, so all of my proofing times will change with the seasons. 
From the time I finish mixing the dough, till the time it goes into the fridge, it’s about 5 hours of proofing time. Once it’s in the fridge I keep it in there for at least 14 hours. Sometimes I push it longer depending on what’ going on in my morning. I just like to do my bakes early in the morning which is why I don’t let them go longer. 
 
What do you use to score your bread? 
I’m super cheap and have not been able to bring myself to buy a proper lame yet, so I have been using a utility blade for most of my bakes. Eventually, I’ll invest in one of the many amazing lames that exist out there. 
 
How do you bake your typical bread? (Dutch oven, steam oven, loaf pan…) 
I bake most of my loaves inside a 5 qtr. cast iron, non-enameled, Dutch oven. I preheat my oven to 500f / 260c for about an hour with the Dutch oven inside. My oven tells me it’s hot after about a half hour, but I want the Dutch oven to be as hot as possible so I just let it preheat longer. Once the oven is hot enough, I take the dough out of the refrigerator and turn it our onto parchment paper and score it. I transfer it into the bottom of the Dutch oven, cover with the lid and put into the oven for 25 minutes. After that I take off the lid and turn the temperature down to about 450f / 232c and cook it for 18 to 23 more minutes.  I base my decision on when to pull it from the oven based on the color of the crust. If I want a darker loaf, I just leave it in a bit longer. 
 
What is your favorite use for sourdough discard? 
Sourdough Pancakes!!! Without a doubt! I found a recipe online from Taste of Lizzy and it’s the single best pancake recipe I’ve ever tasted. Do yourself a favor and check out their site and make it!
 
I keep a mason jar in my fridge and all week I add my discard to it. By Friday I have plenty of discard to make something fun with. 
 
How do you see yourself expanding your sourdough skills in the future? Are there new skill you want to master?
I’m reading books by bakers like Vanessa Kimbell from The Sourdough School, and Tartine Bread by Chad Robertson. I also plan to invest in a home grain mill in the next year or so. I started looking at ancient grain seeds online at one point when I ambitiously contemplated trying to grow my own grains, but I live in a city and really don’t think that’s the kind of thing that works well in planter boxes. 
 
Are there any projects you are working on that you’d like to share with us?
Excuse me while I self promote our site for a moment, but I’m excited for things to come. Alexander and I have worked hard on a redesign plan sourdoughbake.com. You will be seeing a redesigned homepage soon, followed by a full redesign of the baker's percentage calculator as well as a chat platform and more. 
 
What advice do you have for new bakers? 
I guess I’d say, relax and just enjoy the learning process. Don’t focus on your crust, crumb or scoring and don’t compare you bread to that which you see on Instagram. When you do your first bake, find ONE single guide and follow it. If you watch 9 hours of videos on how to make a loaf of bread and then you try to combine all the things you saw, you are likely going to feel frustrated when your bread does not turn out like you expect. Find one guide who you like and mimic what they do. Learn in increments and explore on your own terms. Ultimately, remember the thing that makes a successful loaf of bread is that you enjoy eating it.
