Interview With a Baker - Tammy Jones

In our “Interview with a Baker” series, we ask artisanal bakers from across the globe with different backgrounds to answer twenty questions about their experience with sourdough and baking in general. The questions the bakers answer range on topics from their baking history to specific techniques they use in their bread making process.
 
Hello, Tammy. Tell us a little about yourself, your background and who you are.
Hello! I'm Tammy, 49 years old. I work as an environmental attorney in Southern California for a major utility company, but I spend my weekends baking sourdough. I've been cooking and baking since I can remember, but sourdough is really something I am passionate about and I love to work with local millers to the extent I can. Keeping things local, and sharing with my community is something very important to me.
 
How can we find you on social media? 
On Instagram, my profile is @breadbakinggirl. I am also part of the Speakeasy Bakery Community here in Santa Clarita.
Do you have a website or blog or perhaps a video sharing platform you'd like to share? 
Nothing other than above.    
 
Where in the world are you located?
Stevenson Ranch, California, US.
 
What inspired you to explore sourdough? 
My dad is a diabetic, but loves bread. We found that when he eats sourdough, he doesn't have blood sugar spikes so I just kept baking it. That and I was raised in part near San Francisco, so I grew up loving that sourdough "tang". Also, I'm borderline obsessed with following such great bakers on Instagram and each time I see something new I find I just have to try it myself!
 
How long have you been making bread, and specifically sourdough bread?  
On and off for five years, but intensely and weekly for a year now.
 
What has been the hardest part of learning how to make sourdough for you? 
Figuring out the proofing and finding space in my fridge to hold everything! I love to bake in large amounts, usually 8-10 loaves at a time, and I don't have the kitchen appliances to support my obsession. I've been living with a broken oven for two years now, so having to juggle with limited oven and fridge space has been a challenge. Also, shaping batards! I have no idea why, but boules seem to come more naturally to me.
 
What is your personal style with regards to making sourdough bread? 
I like to keep my bread fairly rustic, as I do my cooking. I find that my bread baking style has naturally conformed to my cooking style. I'm not overly artistic, although I did spend a year or so painting, so you'll probably never see beautifully artistic scoring from me, but I know it tastes good. I'm also really passionate about working with different grains, particularly ancient grains, so my bread always contains a lot more "wheatiness" than others.
 
What’s your routine for refreshing and maintaining your starter? 
I keep two starters regularly, a white and a wheat. Always in the back of my fridge in Weck jars. We like our sourdough more on the sour side, so the fridge is my friend. I always mix and bulk on Saturday and bake on Sunday. So on Wednesday I take "thing" and "wheat thing" ("sweet thing is my chocolate starter that I pull out during the holidays) out of the fridge and start feeding them twice a day and three times on Friday to get them super active for the weekend. I generally keep about 200 grams of each total.
 
How soon before mixing your dough do you start your autolyse?
Depends on the grain, some grains like spelt, don't necessarily require a long autolyse. But generally at least two hours, often as long as four.
 
What is your favorite grain to work with?  
Right now Einkorn. I adore the nutty flavor and the tangy aftertaste. I also love how versatile it is.
 
What is your prefered technique for developing gluten in your dough once it’s mixed together? 
I always do stretch and folds every 30 minutes for the first couple hours, and do a couple coil folds after each stretch and fold. I love coil folds, there is something very relaxing about them! I also do a pretty vigorous kind of "pseudo" slap and fold in the bowl right after mixing, until the dough starts to smooth out a bit and gets sticky.
 
Do you prefer the ambient or retarded method of baking? 
Always retarded for me, again, the sour.
 
How long do you let your dough proof for? 
It depends on the grain, whole grains ferment a lot faster, but typically I bulk for about four hours after mixing and then divide, shape and put in the fridge for anywhere between 12 and 30 hours.
 
What do you use to score your bread? 
Tyler's Wire Monkey UFO lame!!
 
How do you bake your typical bread? (Dutch oven, steam oven, loaf pan…) 
Almost always in a Lodge combo cooker. Unless I'm baking baguettes or large batards, then on a pizza stone with lots of steam. Sometimes I'll pour hot water over dish towels and put them in a roasting pan under the pizza oven to really steam things up.
 
What is your favorite use for sourdough discard? 
Sourdough pancakes. I always have a batch of batter next to the stove on the weekends.
 
How do you see yourself expanding your sourdough skills in the future? Are there new skill you want to master?
I'm going to try lamination next, plus I've been thinking about milling my own grains. I'm really fortunate to have Grist & Toll just a couple miles from my office. They are one of the only millers local to this area, and lucky for me, one of the best around in general, so I haven't had to mill my own, but I might give it a try one day.
 
Are there any projects you are working on that you’d like to share with us?
Not yet.
 
 
What advice do you have for new bakers? 
Learn your grains, they aren't all the same and need to be treated differently. Remember, there isn't really such a thing as "white" flour, it's all wheat, just extracted differently. Also, keep detailed logs of what works and what doesn't. I've been keeping a baking diary for years, and refer to it constantly. Finally, just learn to trust your instincts, and get a good thermometer. Time and temperature are the two most important variables in sourdough.
